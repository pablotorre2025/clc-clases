Module 1

Theological Foundations of Christian Conduct

MODULE 1: THEOLOGICAL FOUNDATIONS OF CHRISTIAN CONDUCT

INTRODUCTION

Christian conduct does not arise from arbitrary rules or empty moralism. Rather, it emerges from a deep understanding of who God is, what He has done for us through Christ, and how the Holy Spirit empowers us to live in a way that reflects His character. This module establishes the theological foundations that support all authentic Christian conduct.

SECTION 1.1: GRACE AS THE BASIS OF TRANSFORMATION

Understanding Grace

Grace is expressed in Hebrew through two main terms: chesed (חֶסֶד), which means "grace, mercy, steadfast love, compassion", and chen (חֵן), which means "grace, kindness, goodness". In Greek, charis (χάρις) conveys "grace, favor, kindness, benevolence".

Each term reveals a different dimension of the concept:

  • Chesed: Emphasizes the unwavering loyalty and constant love of God, regardless of our behavior.
  • Chen: Highlights the kindness and favor that is freely granted, without prior merit.
  • Charis: In the New Testament, it represents the unmerited favor that God extends through Christ.
The Nature of Divine Grace

Grace is the kind or merciful conduct that a more powerful person exercises over another. In the Christian context, this means that God, being infinitely superior, chooses to treat us with favor that we have neither earned nor deserved.

God's grace towards humanity arises from the fullness of His being. He is a God of grace. When God appeared to Moses, He declared His name, Yahweh, the I AM, as the sum of His eternal being. This nature includes His grace: "The Lord, the Lord, the compassionate and gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness" (Exodus 34:6).

Distinction: Common Grace vs. Special Grace

Understanding these two types of grace is fundamental to understanding how God acts in the world and in our lives:

  • Common Grace: Common grace is all the favor God shows to humanity that falls short of salvation. This includes the blessings we all experience: rain, sun, health, family, opportunities. Even unbelievers receive common grace.
  • Special Grace (Saving Grace): Special grace is saving grace, the work of the Spirit in applying Christ's atonement to human beings. Special grace is pure grace. It does not arise from anything that may reside in the unworthy recipient. It is only by grace that one is saved, through faith, which is also a gift of grace (Ephesians 2:7–9).
Grace as an Act, Not a State

An important distinction emerges from the analysis of the Greek term. Grace does not mean "endued us with grace", nor "made us worthy of love", but rather, as the Revision indicates, "grace—which he freely bestowed". Grace is an act of God, not a state in which He places us.

This has profound implications: grace is not a quality we possess, but a continuous action of God toward us. It is not something we earn or maintain; it is something we constantly receive.

Reflective Questions

  • How would your life change if you truly understood that God's grace does not depend on your performance?
  • How do you experience common grace in your daily life?
  • How should the special grace of salvation motivate your conduct?

SECTION 1.2: SALVATION AS INTEGRAL TRANSFORMATION

The Gospel as Transforming Power

The wonderful and potent nature of the gospel is noted in several affirmations. The gospel saves. The gospel offers forgiveness to those who accept it and rescues them from the punishment of sin, which is eternal death. It frees us from the power of sin, and one day, when we are with God, we will be saved from the very presence of sin.

The gospel is not simply a message of past forgiveness; it is a power that continues to transform our lives in the present and promises us future deliverance.

Justification vs. Sanctification: Two Aspects of Salvation

Although these terms are often used interchangeably, they represent different but complementary aspects of salvation:

  • Justification: Sanctification by God is placed before man's faith. The dual aspect of salvation is presented as follows: God's grace in the first instance sanctifies us (that is, sets us apart in His eternal purposes as saints for Himself); and our faith, by God's gift, lays hold of salvation (2 Thessalonians 2:13; 1 Peter 1:2).
  • Sanctification: In Christian theology, a distinction is sometimes made between justification and sanctification, where justification refers to having saving faith and sanctification refers to the process of gradual purification from sin and progressive spiritual growth that should characterize the life of the believer.
Sanctification: Positional and Progressive

A crucial aspect is that sanctification has two dimensions:

  1. Positional Sanctification (Instantaneous): Christ, who once and for all consecrates His people to God (bringing them near to Him as a result) and to eternal glory, by having consecrated Himself for them, being made perfect (as their atoning sacrifice) through suffering. God, by His electing love, through the finished work of Christ, perfectly sanctifies them to the service of God and to heaven once and for all; afterward they are progressively sanctified through the transforming Spirit.
  2. Progressive Sanctification (Continuous): The clear teaching of Scripture is that believers must grow and change continually (2 Corinthians 3:18; Romans 8:28–29; Colossians 1:28–29; Ephesians 4:11–16, 22–24) and not remain immature (Hebrews 5:11–6:4).
The Transforming Purpose of the Gospel

God's calling is holy. The gospel is not only meant to forgive sins, and secure our eternal destiny, but it has the purpose of producing holiness in the believer. The gospel transforms lives.

God tells His people: "Be holy, because I am holy" (Leviticus 11:44–45, 19:2, 1 Peter 1:15–16). Holiness is not an option for the Christian; it is the fundamental purpose of salvation.

The Right Motivation for Obedience

A common mistake is thinking we must obey to maintain our salvation. Salvation does not depend on human effort. The emphasis is that salvation is by grace, not by works. Redemption is not earned by human merit (Ephesians 2:8–10, Titus 3:4–5).

Instead, we should be motivated to obey God out of love and gratitude, longing to be conformed to the image of Jesus Christ. The Christian must manifest the holy and glorious character of God to the world.

Reflective Questions

  • Do you understand your salvation as a past event, a present process, or a future promise? Why is it important to see it as all three?
  • How does understanding that you are sanctified in Christ (positionally) affect your motivation to grow spiritually?
  • What is the difference between obeying to be saved and obeying because you are already saved?

SECTION 1.3: FUNDAMENTAL DOCTRINES FOR CHRISTIAN CONDUCT

Christian conduct is built upon six fundamental doctrines that intertwine to form a coherent system of theological understanding:

1. Hamartiology: Recognition of Sinful Nature
Definition: The doctrine of sin and its nature.
The fundamental need to recognize our fallen and sinful condition: without this element the experience of grace cannot exist in personal life, since the only way to seek a solution to a problem is by recognizing that there is one, and the problem — or its primary source — consists in our sinfulness.
Implication for Conduct: We cannot change what we do not acknowledge. Christian conduct begins with the honest admission of our inability to live rightly on our own. Sin contaminated humanity and separated us from our purpose. From that point on, every human being is born apart from God and unable to draw near by their own means (Romans 3:23).
2. Christology: Christ as Model and Mediator
Definition: The doctrine of the person and work of Jesus Christ.
The need for faith in the Son as the means of salvation: Scripture is very clear when it states that the only means by which human beings can attain salvation from the power and dominion of sin and the deserved eternal condemnation is by placing faith in the person and work of the Lord Jesus.
Implication for Conduct: Jesus Christ is the perfect example of moral, pure, and holy conduct, while at the same time drawing near to the sinner to set them free. Our conduct must reflect that of Christ: holiness combined with compassion. The model of sanctification is Christ. When we face moral decisions, we ask: “What would Jesus do?”
3. Bibliology: The Word of God as Authority
Definition: The doctrine of the inspiration, authority, and interpretation of Scripture.
The personal need to study, analyze, and responsibly apply the Word of God in personal life: the Bible, apart from personal experience, is the only means by which humanity can consciously and intelligently comprehend and enjoy God’s unmerited favor in every matter of personal life.
Implication for Conduct: The Bible is the divine means for the guidance, correction, and strength necessary to live a life of holiness pleasing to God. Christian conduct is grounded in the Word, not in personal opinions or cultural trends.
4. Pneumatology: The Holy Spirit as Enabler
Definition: The doctrine of the person and work of the Holy Spirit.
The divine person of the Spirit educating, guiding, shaping, and preparing us to walk in His grace: grace becomes a describable experience at the moment of regeneration, meaning, the new birth marks the experience fully, satisfactorily, and positively, progressively providing conformity to the image of the Son (Galatians 5:16–24). Divine grace as an experience aims to lead the recipient to walk according to divine commandments (Romans 8:1–2).
Implication for Conduct: The Holy Spirit is the one who provides the self-control necessary to manage impulses. We are not capable of living righteously by our own strength; we depend on the power of the Spirit. Spiritual growth (sanctification) is the work of God in which the believer has the responsibility to participate actively and with effort (Philippians 2:12–13; 1 Corinthians 9:24–27; Ephesians 6:10–12; 2 Timothy 4:6–7).
5. Theology: Knowledge of the Character of God
Definition: The doctrine of the nature, attributes, and actions of God.
The clear understanding of God’s motivations, inclinations, and actions toward creation and His purposes: those who have experienced God’s gift in their lives can understand, in a limited way through the external evidence displayed in creation and the internal evidence revealed in the Bible, what God’s plan and purpose for His creation is. It should be noted that God cannot be fully studied or comprehended through the lens of our finite minds; therefore, we can conclude that the means God has sovereignly chosen to display His grace are adequate to benefit us even within our limitations.
Implication for Conduct: Christian conduct reflects the character of God. If we understand that God is just, we will be just; if we understand that He is merciful, we will be merciful.
6. Human Responsibility
Definition: The doctrine of human nature and moral responsibility.
Human beings and their moral responsibilities before God and society. Although we are saved by grace, we are responsible for our actions.
Implication for Conduct: The presence of personal sin, behaviors, inclinations, desires, and ethics. There must be a clear understanding of these realities in personal life, so that there is awareness not only of one’s responsibilities, but also of the benefits of doing what is right and the consequences of not doing it.
How These Doctrines Intertwine

Imagine these six doctrines as the threads of a rope:

  1. Hamartiology identifies the problem (we are sinners)
  2. Christology provides the solution (Christ died for us)
  3. Bibliology teaches us how to live (the Word guides us)
  4. Pneumatology empowers us to live (the Spirit strengthens us)
  5. Theology inspires us to live (we imitate the character of God)
  6. Anthropology holds us responsible to live (we are accountable for our actions)

Together, these doctrines create a solid foundation for authentic Christian conduct.

Reflective Questions

  • Which of these six doctrines is the least clear to you? How could you study it more deeply?
  • How does each of these doctrines specifically affect your daily conduct?
  • What would happen if you ignored one of these doctrines? (For example, what would happen if you ignored Pneumatology?)

SECTION 1.4: THE ORIGINAL PURPOSE OF HUMAN BEINGS

Created for a Specific Purpose

Before we can understand how we should live, we must understand why we were created. God created humanity for Himself, so that we would give Him glory and live in intimate communion with Him. In other words, He created us for a specific, consecrated, holy purpose (Romans 11:36; Colossians 1:16; Genesis 1:26–27).

This is revolutionary: we were not created for ourselves, but for God. Our existence has a purpose that transcends our personal desires.

When the Bible says we were created "in the image of God," it is not speaking of physical appearance. It speaks of something far deeper: God gave us reason to think, will to choose, emotions to feel, and —most importantly— the capacity to have a real relationship with Him. No other creature in all of creation received that. A tree cannot worship. An animal cannot obey out of conviction. Only human beings were designed to know God, respond to Him, and live in communion with Him. That is why sin was not merely a moral failure —it was the breaking of the very bond for which we were made.

The Fall and Restoration

Sin contaminated humanity and separated us from our purpose. From that point on, every human being is born separated from God and unable to draw near by their own means (Romans 3:23).

The consequences of that fall were not only spiritual. Sin produced something concrete in human behavior: human beings stopped living for God and began living for themselves. Selfishness, the pursuit of approval, boundless ambition, the inability to serve without expecting something in return —all of that is a direct consequence of losing the original compass. The human being who was meant to be a mirror of God's glory became someone who consumes everything around them to feed their own emptiness.

That is why, through the work of redemption that Christ accomplished, the human being is restored to the position of being set apart for God. We can now fulfill our original purpose (Ephesians 1:5–12). As Scripture declares: "For we are God's masterpiece. He has created us anew in Christ Jesus, so we can do the good things he planned for us long ago" (Ephesians 2:10, NLT).

Christian conduct, then, is not a set of restrictions — it is the restoration of our original purpose: to glorify God and live in communion with Him.

Ongoing Practical Sanctification

While we remain in this life, the believer will be in a continuous process of practical sanctification — that is, separating from the world and from sin in order to consecrate every area of life to God (1 Peter 1:15–16).

This means that Christian conduct is not something we achieve and complete; it is a continuous process of turning away from sin and dedicating ourselves more fully to God. Practical sanctification is not just a doctrinal position — it has shape and weight in the day-to-day:

  • At work: It means being honest when no one is watching, not just when the boss is present. It means giving one hundred percent even when the pay is unfair, because we work for God and not just for people (Colossians 3:23).
  • In marriage and family: It means choosing to serve first at the end of a ten-hour day, even when exhaustion invites the opposite. Holiness is also seen in how we speak to our spouse when we are worn out.
  • In what we consume: It means evaluating what enters through our eyes and ears —social media, entertainment, conversations— in light of our purpose to glorify God, not just what "feels good."
Designed for Glory

The motivation to live differently comes from understanding the human being's original "factory design." Humanity was not created to live for itself or to satisfy selfish desires, but to reflect the glory of God and live in communion with Him.

Biblical illustration (Paul): The apostle Paul is the clearest example in the New Testament. Before knowing Christ, Saul lived for himself and for his own religious glory: he persecuted the church, accumulated credentials, and took pride in his lineage. After his encounter with Christ on the road to Damascus, he voluntarily gave it all up and declared: "Yes, everything else is worthless when compared with the infinite value of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord" (Philippians 3:8, NLT). Saul did not change his agenda — he abandoned it completely — because he discovered what he had been created for.

Biblical illustration (Joseph): Joseph in Egypt is another powerful example. He worked as a slave, was tempted, and was unjustly imprisoned. And in each of those situations the text repeats the same phrase: "the Lord was with Joseph" (Genesis 39:2, 21, 23). His purpose of glorifying God did not depend on his circumstances — it depended on whose he was. In the darkest corner of his life, Joseph continued to be a mirror. That is exactly what it means to have been created for a purpose that transcends the conditions of the moment.

Illustration: It is like a mirror specifically designed to capture sunlight and project it into a dark corner; its reason for existing is not to draw attention to itself, but to the source of light.

The Effect of Sin

Sin clouded that "lens" of the human being, transforming us from a mirror that reflects upward into a "black hole" that absorbs everything toward its own ego.

Illustration: A high-end camera whose lens is so smeared with grease that instead of capturing the beauty of the landscape, it only produces distorted, dark images focused on the internal grime.

It is important to note that this "black hole" is not only the problem of those who live far from God. Believers can fall into it as well. We do it when we serve in the church to be recognized, when we seek people's approval more than God's, when our worship becomes a performance for others. We do it when we compare our ministry to someone else's, when it bothers us that someone else receives the credit. In those moments, the lens has become dirty again. Practical sanctification is precisely the process of recognizing that grime and allowing God to cleanse it once more.

Restoration Through Grace

The ultimate goal of grace is to cleanse and restore that lens so that the human being can once again fulfill their original purpose. God is not adding a new function to the human being — He is returning the function for which we were already designed.

As Scripture declares: "So all of us who have had that veil removed can see and reflect the glory of the Lord. And the Lord — who is the Spirit — makes us more and more like him as we are changed into his glorious image" (2 Corinthians 3:18, NLT). The human being designed as a mirror, clouded by sin, is transformed back into what he was always meant to be as he gazes upon the glory of the Lord. And the agent of that transformation is not willpower or moral effort: it is the Holy Spirit. Practical sanctification is not a list of rules we keep on our own — it is the Spirit of God working from within, cleaning the lens, restoring the original design.

Reflective Questions

  • What would change if this week, before making every important decision —at work, with your family, in what you consume— you asked yourself: "Does this bring me closer to or further from my purpose of glorifying God?"
  • Think of a specific area of your life —work, marriage, social media, finances— that you have not yet fully placed in God's hands. What has kept you from consecrating it?
  • Many of us are carrying weeks of exhaustion, financial pressure, or family distance. How does that reality change when you live it from the purpose of glorifying God rather than just surviving the day?

SECTION 1.5: GRACE AS MOTIVATION FOR OBEDIENCE

The Cycle of Grace

Grace does not only save us; it also motivates us to live righteously. This is a continuous cycle:

  1. We receive grace (God forgives us and accepts us)
  2. We experience gratitude (we recognize what God has done)
  3. We are motivated to obey (we want to please God)
  4. We live with transformed conduct (our actions reflect our gratitude)
  5. We experience more grace (God continues to work in us)
Avoiding Two Extremes

Extreme 1: Legalism. Some believe they must obey in order to be saved or to remain saved. This turns obedience into a heavy burden and denies grace.

Extreme 2: Licentiousness. Others believe that since we are saved by grace, conduct does not matter. This abuses grace and denies the transformation that must accompany salvation.

The Biblical Balance: Being saved by grace does not mean that the believer will continue on the same path of sinfulness. If a person persists in the same disobedience, they are not showing the fruit of repentance and regeneration.

Grace Restores Nature

Special grace renews believers from within, redeeming them from the guilt and punishment of sin, cleansing them from the contamination of sin (Philippians 1:6) and giving them spiritual gifts (Galatians 5:22–23). Therefore, God’s special grace not only saves sinners from the penalty of sin, but also renews and restores what was corrupted by the fall. Grace restores nature.

This is hopeful: grace does not only forgive us; it also restores us to what we were created to be.

Reflective Questions

  • How do you experience God’s grace in your daily life?
  • What role does gratitude play in your motivation to obey God?
  • In what areas have you experienced the restoration of grace in your life?

SECTION 1.6: SYNTHESIS AND APPLICATION

Summary of Theological Foundations

The theological foundations of Christian conduct rest on these interconnected truths:

  1. Grace is the foundation — We do not earn salvation; we receive it as an unmerited gift
  2. Salvation is transformative — The gospel does not only forgive us; it changes us
  3. Six doctrines guide us — Hamartiology, Christology, Bibliology, Pneumatology, Theology, and Anthropology
  4. We have a purpose — We were created to glorify God and live in communion with Him
  5. Grace motivates us — We obey out of love and gratitude, not out of obligation
KEY PASSAGES TO MEMORIZE
  • Romans 12:2 — “Do not conform to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind”
  • Ephesians 2:8–10 — “For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith… we are God’s handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works”
  • 2 Timothy 1:9 — “He has saved us and called us to a holy life — not because of anything we have done but because of his own purpose and grace”
  • 1 Peter 1:15–16 — “But just as he who called you is holy, so be holy in all you do; for it is written: ‘Be holy, because I am holy’”
  • Colossians 1:28–29 — “He is the one we proclaim, admonishing and teaching everyone with all wisdom, so that we may present everyone fully mature in Christ”
RESOURCES FOR DEEPER STUDY

1. "Backbone" Books (Full Reading)

If you want to understand how theology translates into life, these three books are mandatory:

  • Romans (Especially chs. 1 to 12): It is the most comprehensive treatise on sin (Hamartiology), justification (Christology/Grace), and life in the Spirit (Pneumatology). Chapter 12 is the perfect bridge to conduct.
  • Ephesians: Perfectly divides the Christian's position (chs. 1-3: what God did for us) and practice (chs. 4-6: how we should live).
  • Galatians: Fundamental for understanding the difference between living by Grace and falling into Legalism.

2. Key Chapters by Doctrine (For deep study)

To master the 6 doctrines mentioned in the text, study these specific chapters:

On Grace and the Character of God

  • Exodus 34: The revelation of God's name and character (merciful and gracious).
  • Psalm 103: A beautiful description of divine grace and compassion.
  • John 1: Grace and truth incarnated in Jesus.

On Sin and Necessity

  • Genesis 3: The origin of the fall and the loss of original purpose.
  • Romans 3: The universality of sin and human inability.

On Salvation and Identity

  • John 3: The new birth and God's initiative.
  • 2 Corinthians 5: The work of reconciliation and our new identity as "new creations".
  • Titus 3:1-7: A perfect summary of how grace saves us for good works.

On Transformation and the Spirit

  • John 14 to 16: Jesus' promises about the Holy Spirit as Comforter and Guide.
  • Galatians 5: The contrast between the acts of the flesh and the Fruit of the Spirit.
  • Romans 8: Life in the Spirit and victory over sin.

On the Word and Authority

  • Psalm 119: The love for the Word as a guide for conduct.
  • 2 Timothy 3: The inspiration of Scripture and its usefulness for “teaching, rebuking, correcting”.
